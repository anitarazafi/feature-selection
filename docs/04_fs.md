DE is less common in feature selection literature than GA or PSO, which actually works in your favor — it's a more novel contribution


The comparison between SHAP and LIME is itself a contribution — you can answer "do these two XAI approaches agree on which features matter for TC landfall?"
If they agree → strengthens the validity of your feature selection. If they disagree → that's an interesting finding worth discussing
The argument is that they represent two philosophically different XAI approaches — SHAP gives a theoretically grounded global view of feature importance, while LIME gives a model-agnostic local approximation. Comparing them lets you answer: "Do globally important features agree with locally important ones?" — which is itself an interesting finding for your time series dataset.

"To our knowledge, this is one of the first studies to compare SHAP and LIME as feature selection methods on IBTrACS time-series data"

LIME is slow on large datasets since it perturbs each sample individually. With your ~300k rows you won't run LIME on the full dataset, you'd run it on a representative sample.

Traditional FS key findings:
1. MI k=10 and RFE k=10 are jointly the best — both achieve RF AUC of 0.9706, beating baseline (0.9299) by a significant margin. Just 10 features outperform 63 features — this is your headline finding.
2. Chi2 completely fails at k=10 — AUC drops to 0.82-0.68, MLP gets zero F1. This is a strong contrast to MI and RFE at the same k, showing that chi2 is sensitive to aggressive feature reduction on this dataset.
3. Clear pattern: performance peaks at k=10 then degrades — true for all three methods. Adding more features beyond 10 hurts rather than helps, confirming significant redundancy in the original 63 features.
4. MI vs RFE tradeoff — both perform similarly at k=10, but MI takes 31s vs RFE's 25s. However RFE is a wrapper method using model feedback while MI is a pure filter — the fact they agree on feature importance strengthens both results.
5. Chi2 needs at least k=30 to be viable — below that it breaks down, especially for MLP. Worth noting as a limitation of chi2 on meteorological data with mixed scales.
6. MLP benefits most from feature selection — at baseline MLP AUC was 0.9089, with MI k=10 it reaches 0.9109 and with RFE k=10 it reaches 0.9256. Confirms your earlier hypothesis that neural networks are more sensitive to noisy features.

"Chi2 with k=10 caused complete failure in MLP (F1=0), indicating that chi2 selects features that are statistically dependent on the target but insufficient for neural network learning at aggressive reduction levels. This highlights a key limitation of chi2 as a filter method for imbalanced time-series data."
